Vasu Dipakkumar Pipwala

Hi, I’m glad you stopped by — welcome!

I’m an astrophysics researcher working with observational data, computation, and statistics to understand what stars and stellar systems can tell us about galaxies. My research background is rooted in stellar populations and variable stars (Classical Cepheids, Type-II Cepheids, and RR Lyrae), and their use in studying Galactic structure, chemical enrichment, and the distance scale.

Looking ahead, I am preparing to transition toward Galactic and extragalactic dynamics at the PhD level. I’m especially interested in how phase-space structure, orbital evolution, and dynamical modeling can be used to connect observed stellar systems (such as streams, halos, and satellites) to the underlying gravitational potential, and in turn to constrain the distribution and role of dark matter in shaping the assembly history of galaxies.

Research Highlights

Bayesian Distance Calibration with Type-II Cepheids

During my M.Sc. at Heidelberg University, I investigated the use of Type-II Cepheids as extragalactic standard candles. I developed robust Bayesian regression frameworks to calibrate Period–Luminosity and Period–Wesenheit relations in the LMC and M31, explicitly accounting for heteroscedastic uncertainties and contamination.

Rather than relying on ordinary least squares, I emphasized uncertainty propagation, posterior validation, and statistical consistency checks. This work demonstrated that Type-II Cepheids can serve as reliable Population-II distance indicators in galaxies lacking young stellar populations, with direct relevance for JWST- and LSST-era observations.

NLTE Spectral Modeling & Chemical Evolution

As a Research Assistant at the University of Rome Tor Vergata and INAF-OAR, I conducted high-resolution spectroscopic studies of Galactic RR Lyrae stars to probe early Milky Way chemical enrichment.

I designed and implemented automated, reproducible Python pipelines for multi-instrument spectroscopic datasets and developed a wrapper for physically constrained NLTE synthetic spectral modeling. I also applied Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) to decompose stellar spectra into interpretable components, separating continuum structure, line features, and noise-dominated modes.

NASA ADS

Physics-Driven Stellar Streams & Graph Neural Networks

Motivated by my growing interest in galactic dynamics and dark matter, I designed a controlled numerical experiment to study whether halo geometry emerges as an organizing principle of stellar-stream phase-space structure.

I generated a factorial ensemble of globular-cluster streams in spherical, oblate, and prolate halo potentials while varying progenitor mass. Each stream was represented as a graph in six-dimensional phase space and analyzed using a Graph Neural Network (GNN) as a representation-learning diagnostic, not a predictive model.

The central question was: do streams cluster in embedding space according to halo geometry rather than progenitor mass? By framing machine learning as a controlled dynamical experiment, this project bridges astrophysical theory and modern geometric ML.
